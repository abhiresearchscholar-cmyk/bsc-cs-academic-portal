00 The very first question

What actually is a "computer"?

Strip away the screens and the keyboards, and every computer — a supercomputer, your laptop, a calculator, an ATM — does exactly the same simple job, in the same four steps. In fact, the word "computer" is older than electronics itself.

💡
Did you know? Before machines existed, the word "computer" described a person whose job was to do calculations by hand — often for astronomy or navy navigation tables. Rooms full of human "computers" once did the same work your phone now does in a fraction of a second.
🍳
Think of a kitchen. You bring in raw ingredients (Input), a cook follows a recipe to prepare them (Processing), a finished dish comes out (Output), and any leftovers go in the fridge for later (Storage). A computer works on data exactly the same way.

One more piece of the puzzle: everything a computer works with is called data (raw facts and figures — a number, a word, a photo). Once the computer processes that data into something meaningful and useful, we call it information. A list of numbers is data; "your average marks are 78%" is information.
TAKEAWAY · A computer = Input → Process → Output, with Storage supporting every step. This exact cycle runs inside every computer on Earth, big or small — and it turns raw data into useful information.
01 What's happening inside

Deep down, a computer only ever knows two things: ON and OFF.

Open up any computer chip and, underneath everything, you'll find millions (even billions) of tiny electronic switches, just like a light switch on your wall. Each one can be in exactly one of two states — nothing more complicated than that.

Interactive · a single switchstate: OFF

The switch is OFF. No current flows. We give this state a name: 0.

💡
The big idea: Computer engineers simply call OFF "0" and ON "1". That's it — that's the whole mystery of "binary" you may have heard of. Two states, two digits.
🐛
Did you know? The word "bug", used for a computer error, may come from 1947 — engineers working on the Harvard Mark II computer found an actual moth trapped in one of its switches, causing a malfunction! They taped it into their logbook, calling it "the first actual case of a bug being found."
TAKEAWAY · A switch has 2 states: OFF (0) and ON (1). Chips are just millions of these switches. This 2-symbol system is called binary.
02 From switches to numbers

Line up 8 switches, and you can count all the way to 255.

One switch can only say "yes" or "no". But put 8 of them in a row — called a byte — and by turning different ones on, you can represent any number from 0 to 255. Try it yourself below: click the switches and watch the number build.

Did you know? "Binary" simply means "having two parts" — like a bicycle has two wheels. A computer's bit (binary digit) is the smallest possible piece of information: one switch, one yes-or-no answer.
TAKEAWAY · 8 switches (1 byte) can represent 256 different values (0–255). This is how all data — text, images, sound — is ultimately stored.
03 From switches to words

A computer has never seen a letter in its life. So how does it show you your name?

Here's the trick: everyone simply agrees on a code that assigns a number to every letter. The capital letter "A" is agreed to be the number 65. "B" is 66. And so on. Once a letter is a number, and a number is a pattern of switches — we're back to something a computer can store! This particular agreement is called ASCII.

Interactive · type your name and see it in "computer language"
✨
Did you know? Your first name probably takes up less than 10 bytes of storage — smaller than a single text message emoji! Yet a single photo from your phone can be millions of bytes, because it's really thousands of tiny coloured dots, each one also stored as a number.
📖
Same trick, every time. Colours in a photo, notes in a song, frames in a video — all of it is just numbers, agreed upon, stored as switches. Once you understand the letter-to-number trick above, you understand the deepest secret of every computer file that has ever existed.
TAKEAWAY · A computer only ever sees numbers. Letters, colours, and sounds all become numbers first — through agreed codes like ASCII — before they're stored as binary switches.

But accuracy has a catch — meet "GIGO"

A computer's maths is never wrong. But it can only work with what you give it. Feed it good information, you get a good answer. Feed it nonsense, it gives you nonsense back, just as confidently. This is called GIGO — Garbage In, Garbage Out.

06 Not all computers look alike

From a machine that fills a hall, to one that fits on your wrist.

"Computer" is really an umbrella term for a whole family of machines, sorted mainly by their size and power. Tap each one to learn where you'd find it.

Interactive · the computer family
🚗
Think of vehicles. A supercomputer is like a cargo ship — huge, built for one massive job. A smartphone is like a bicycle — small, personal, everywhere. Both are "vehicles"; both are "computers" — just built for very different jobs.

A quick word: analog, digital, and hybrid

There's one more way to sort computers — by whether they measure or count. A traditional needle speedometer measures a smooth, continuous quantity — that's analog. Your phone counts in exact binary digits — that's digital, and it's what "computer" means to almost everyone today. Some special machines, like a hospital heart monitor, do both at once — that's hybrid.

🌡️
Analog

Measures a smooth, continuous signal. E.g. a mercury thermometer, an old speedometer needle.

🔢
Digital

Counts in exact binary digits. E.g. your phone, laptop, digital watch — almost everything today.

🏥
Hybrid

Does both — measures a real-world signal, then processes it digitally. E.g. an ICU heart-rate monitor.

Fun comparison: computer vs. the human brain

Since we're on the subject of computing power, here's some fun perspective — because your own brain is the most extraordinary computer of all.

TAKEAWAY · Supercomputer (fastest, for research) → Mainframe (serves thousands at once) → Personal computer (one person, everyday use) → Embedded computer (hidden inside another device). Plus: analog measures, digital counts, hybrid does both.

09 Why this matters to you

You don't need to code to think like a computer scientist.

Whatever you go on to study — commerce, biology, design, literature — you will spend your working life around computers. Understanding this one lecture's worth of ideas means you'll never feel lost in a conversation about "the cloud", "AI", or "why my laptop is so slow." That confidence is the whole point of this course.

Nothing is magic

Every computer, from a phone to a supercomputer, is just switches, wired together, following the same simple cycle.

You can ask better questions

Knowing the vocabulary — input, output, generations, types — means you can follow (and join) any conversation about tech.

This is just the start

Every future lecture in this course builds on today's ideas: what a computer is, its strengths, its history, and its variety.
