00 The switch

Underneath everything is a tiny switch you control with electricity.

The whole machine is built from one component repeated at unimaginable scale: the transistor. A transistor is a switch with no moving parts — a small voltage on its gate decides whether current is allowed to flow. Gate on, current passes. Gate off, it stops. A modern chip packs tens of billions of these onto a fingernail of silicon — that is what an "IC", an integrated circuit, actually is.

01 Electricity as a signal

On a real wire, 1 and 0 are just high and low voltage.

A wire inside a computer carries a voltage that changes over time. When the voltage is high we read a 1; when it drops low we read a 0. Slice time into equal ticks (that's the job of the clock), read the voltage at each tick, and a single wire streams out a sequence like 10110100. That's it — electricity is the information. 02 One and zero

Two states are all you need. We call them 1 and 0.

One switch = one bit, and one bit can only say two things. Line up eight switches and you get a byte — now, by turning them on and off in patterns, you can count all the way to 255. Each position is worth double the one to its right. Click the switches and watch the number build.

03 Bits become meaning

If numbers are just patterns, then so is everything else.

A number is a pattern of bits, so we simply agree that certain patterns mean other things. In ASCII, capital A is 65, B is 66. Store that number and you've stored a letter. The same move gives us pixels (numbers for colour), audio (numbers for pressure), everything. Type below and watch your words become switches.

04 Logic gates

Wire switches together and they start making decisions.

A computer has to reason with bits. Connect a few transistors in clever arrangements and you get logic gates — tiny circuits that take input bits and produce an output bit by a fixed rule. These are the verbs of the machine. Pick a gate, flip the inputs, and read off the rule.

05 From logic to arithmetic

Two gates, wired just so, can add.

Take an XOR gate (1 when inputs differ) and an AND gate, feed them the same two bits, and you've built a half adder. XOR gives the sum bit; AND gives the carry. In binary, 1 + 1 = 10 — just like 9 + 1 = 10 in decimal. Stack enough of these and you can add any numbers, which is the seed of all computation. Flip the inputs and watch it add.

06 The whole computer

Now zoom all the way out. Here's the machine.

Everything we've built sits inside a handful of parts wired to a shared system bus — the classic layout every computer follows. The CPU does the thinking (with its Control Unit, ALU, and Registers). RAM holds the running program. Storage keeps your files when the power's off. Input and Output connect it to you. 07 The processor's cycle

Inside the CPU is a loop that never stops.

The CPU runs one relentless cycle: fetch the next instruction from memory, decode what it means, execute it, then repeat — billions of times a second. 08 How it understands us

The computer doesn't understand you. Layers of translation do.

You write human-friendly code. The silicon only knows voltage. Between them sits a stack of translators, each turning the layer above into the one below. "Understanding" is just this fall from meaning to metal — and it lands right back at Layer 00, the switch. Translate the sentence down and watch it happen.

10 Why we study this

You just walked the whole machine — from a switch to a sentence.

Everything in your degree lives somewhere on this stack. Circuits and architecture near the bottom. Data structures, algorithms, and languages in the middle. AI, graphics, and networks near the top. Knowing the whole ladder separates someone who uses computers from someone who can build and reason about them.

Nothing is magic

Once you see it's switches all the way down, no layer is intimidating. Every abstraction can be opened.

You can debug anything

When something breaks, you know which layer to look at — the code, the compiler, the memory, or the metal.

You can build the future

New chips, new languages, new AI — all invented by people who understood this stack and pushed one layer further.
