01 The keyboard

Every keystroke is a tiny switch closing.

02 The mouse

A mouse doesn't send a picture — it sends coordinates.

A modern optical mouse uses a tiny camera and LED underneath to photograph the surface thousands of times a second, comparing frames to work out how far and which way it moved. That movement becomes x, y coordinates and button states sent to the computer — nothing more. Move and click in the pad below.

03 The touchscreen

Input and output, sharing the exact same glass.

A touchscreen is special: it is an input device (senses your touch) laid directly over an output device (the display). Two common technologies detect the touch:

TechnologyHow it senses touchTraits
ResistiveTwo flexible layers pressed together at the touch point complete a circuitWorks with any object (finger, gloved hand, stylus) but needs firm pressure; less crisp, cheaper.
CapacitiveSenses the electrical charge of a fingertip touching a glass layer coated with a conductive filmVery responsive to light touch and supports multi-touch (pinch/zoom); needs a conductive touch (bare finger), used in nearly all smartphones.

04 Monitors: LCD & LED

The screen doesn't make its own light — most need a lamp behind it.

An LCD (Liquid Crystal Display) works by twisting liquid crystals to block or let through light from a backlight, with colour filters giving each pixel its red, green or blue. On its own, an LCD panel is just a light valve — it needs that backlight to be visible at all. An LED monitor is not a different picture technology; it is simply an LCD that uses LEDs as its backlight, instead of the older fluorescent (CCFL) tubes. LEDs are slimmer, cooler and more energy-efficient. Toggle the backlight and watch.

Interactive · why a backlight matters
Backlight: ON — the picture is visible

Notice: the liquid-crystal layer is still holding the exact same little face, pixel for pixel, whether the backlight is on or off — but with no backlight, no light gets through, so all you see is black. This is exactly what an LED monitor improves: a better, more efficient backlight, not a different picture layer.

05 Printers: inkjet & laser

Two very different ways to put binary onto paper.

Printers turn the page's bits — of text or an image — back into something physical. The two dominant technologies work in completely different ways. Pick one and press "Print a page" to see how it works.

Inkjet

07 Why it matters

Every device you touch is a translator, at the very edge of the machine.

Input and output devices are where the abstraction stack from Lecture 01 meets the physical world — a fingertip's charge, a spray of ink, a beam of light. Understanding how each device senses or displays information helps you choose the right hardware, debug when input misbehaves, and appreciate just how much translation happens between you and a chip.

Choose the right device

A laser printer for a busy office, an inkjet for photos, a capacitive screen for multi-touch — knowing the tech means knowing the trade-off.

Debug with confidence

Cursor lag, a dead pixel, a smudged print — knowing the mechanism tells you where to look first.

Exam gold

"Compare LCD and LED" and "explain inkjet vs laser" are classic questions — you now have both, in detail.
