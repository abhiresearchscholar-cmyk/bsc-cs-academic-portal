00 Three ways to classify

"Computer" is one word for a whole family of machines.

Before we sort them, notice there isn't one single list — computers are grouped in three different ways depending on what you care about: how they handle data, what job they're built for, or how big and powerful they are. Here's the map for the whole lecture.

01 By the data they handle

Measure, count, or both?

The oldest split: does the machine work with continuous quantities that it measures, or discrete digits that it counts? Pick a type and watch how it represents a signal.

Analog

Analog computer

✋
In class: A mercury thermometer is analog (the column moves smoothly); a digital fever thermometer counts and shows a number. Same job, two philosophies.
TAKEAWAY · Analog measures continuous signals · Digital counts discrete 0/1 · Hybrid takes analog input but processes it digitally.
02 By their purpose

A jack-of-all-trades, or a specialist?

The second lens is simple but important, and examiners love it.

General-purpose

Designed to handle a wide variety of tasks by simply loading different programs — word processing, gaming, browsing, coding. Flexible but not the fastest at any single job.

e.g. your laptop, desktop PC, smartphone

Special-purpose

Built and programmed for one specific task. It cannot be reprogrammed for other jobs, but it does its one job extremely efficiently.

e.g. washing-machine controller, ATM, traffic-signal control, aircraft autopilot
TAKEAWAY · General-purpose = many tasks via different programs · Special-purpose = one dedicated task, highly efficient.
03 By size & power

From a nation's supercomputer to the one in your hand.

The most common classification. As we go from super down to micro, machines get smaller, cheaper, and serve fewer users at once. Step through the four classes.

Relative power
🇮🇳
Indian example: the PARAM series (developed by C-DAC) are India's own supercomputers — used for weather forecasting, research and simulations.
TAKEAWAY · Supercomputer (fastest, science) → Mainframe (big orgs, many users) → Mini/midrange → Microcomputer (personal, single-user).
04 Microcomputers around you

Most "computers" you meet are microcomputers.

The microcomputer category has exploded into many familiar forms — all single-user machines built around one microprocessor.

TAKEAWAY · Desktop, laptop, tablet, smartphone, server, workstation, embedded and wearable devices are all microcomputers.
05 The block diagram

Every computer, super to micro, shares this one plan.

No matter the type, a computer is built from five functional units: Input, Memory, the CPU (which contains the ALU and the Control Unit), and Output — with Secondary Storage alongside. Solid amber arrows carry data; dashed blue arrows carry control signals from the Control Unit. Click a block to learn its job, or press Trace the data to watch information flow through.

07 Why it matters

One plan explains every machine you'll ever use.

The block diagram is the single most important picture in your first year. Every later topic — memory hierarchy, operating systems, computer organisation — is a zoom-in on one of these blocks. Learn to draw it from memory and you have a map for the whole degree.

Pick the right tool

Knowing the types lets you match a machine to a job — you don't buy a mainframe to check email.

A mental model

The five blocks are a lens for debugging: is the problem in input, memory, processing, or output?

Exam gold

"Draw and explain the block diagram" is one of the most common 14-mark questions. Nail it here.
