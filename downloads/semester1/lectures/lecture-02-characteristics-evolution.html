02 Accuracy & GIGO

The computer is always right. That's the problem.

A computer never makes an arithmetic mistake — its answer is exact, every time. So when the output is wrong, the fault is almost always in the input or the instructions we gave it. This is GIGO — Garbage In, Garbage Out. The machine will process nonsense just as flawlessly as it processes truth. Feed it good and bad data and watch.

03 The limits

Powerful, yes — but a computer can't do these.

Balance is important. For all their strengths, computers have no mind of their own. They follow instructions with perfect obedience and zero understanding.

TAKEAWAY · A computer has speed and accuracy, but no common sense, no intent, and no feelings. It only ever does exactly what it's told.

06 Moore's Law

The number of transistors on a chip doubles about every two years.

In 1965 Gordon Moore noticed a pattern that has held for decades: chips keep packing in roughly twice as many transistors every ~2 years. Plotted on a logarithmic scale it becomes an almost straight line — that's exponential growth. Hover the points to see the chips.

09 Why it matters

Knowing the traits and the timeline changes how you build.

Understanding a computer's strengths tells you what to hand it (repetitive, exact, high-volume work) and what to keep for humans (judgement, creativity, care). Understanding the timeline tells you where the field is heading — and how fast.

Design for the strengths

Give computers the speed-and-repetition jobs; design the humans back in where judgement matters.

Respect the limits

GIGO is a career-long lesson: your program is only as good as its data and its logic.

Ride the curve

Moore's Law shaped every product of the last 50 years. Knowing the trend helps you bet on the next one.
